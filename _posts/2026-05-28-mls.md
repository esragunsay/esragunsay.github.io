# Messaging Layer Security (MLS)

Messaging Layer Security (MLS) is becoming the cryptographic foundation of modern secure group 
communication. The protocol was standardized by the IETF as [RFC
9420](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/rfc9420/) and is increasingly positioned as the successor to
many earlier approaches to scalable end-to-end encrypted group messaging. 
Unlike pairwise encrypted systems that extend naturally only to small groups, MLS was
designed for asynchronous communication, large-scale membership changes,
multi-device operation, and long-lived encrypted groups. This blog post explains MLS from the
ground up. The discussion begins with the problem MLS tries to solve and then develops the core
cryptographic structure of the protocol, including TreeKEM, epoch evolution, transcript
synchronization, and the MLS key schedule. After establishing the internal mechanics of MLS do
we move to the advanced topic that forms the central contribution of the referenced paper: external
operations and their impact on post-compromise security. The primary reference for this article is the
paper and presentation material: “ETK: External-Operations TreeKEM and the Security of MLS in
RFC 9420” by Cas Cremers, Mang Zhao, Esra Günsay, and Vera Wesselkamp.
